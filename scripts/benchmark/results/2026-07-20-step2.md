# Step 2 on CPU, T4, and A100: 2026-07-20

CPU fan-out remains the default production placement. The A100 can win for
score-only binary or survival work with many traits, especially when several
chromosome shards share the GPU, but it is not cost-competitive for
quantitative traits or correction-heavy analyses. The distinction matters
because an A100 being faster than eight CPU cores is a much lower bar than an
A100 being worth its price.

The retained CUDA change removes a real bottleneck. Missing-trait score runs
previously expanded each packed genotype into a dense host vector solely to
calculate phenotype-specific allele counts. Those counts now come directly
from the packed block on the GPU. Full 700,000-variant runs improve from 74.69
to 29.96 seconds for eight binary traits and from 90.95 to 40.79 seconds for
eight survival traits. All 16 output files are byte-identical. The same change
reduces a 16,000-variant, 32-trait missing-quantitative control from 8.22 to
6.21 seconds.

## Cost model

Google Cloud's posted prices on 2026-07-20 are $3.673385/hour for an on-demand
`a2-highgpu-1g` in `us-central1`, $0.776944/hour for an on-demand
`n2-standard-16` in `us-west1`, and $0.335008/hour for the same N2 shape as a
Spot VM. The A100 must therefore be 4.73 times faster to break even against
on-demand N2 and 10.97 times faster against Spot N2. These are complete VM
prices; the A2 price includes its A100, 12 vCPUs, and 85 GiB of memory. The N2
is billed as a 16-vCPU VM even though SMT was disabled to expose eight physical
cores during measurement.

The primary comparison below is on-demand A100 versus Spot N2. At 700,000
variants, one A100 process is 9.79 times faster for 32 complete binary traits
and 10.51 times faster for 32 binary traits with 0-10% missingness. Neither
quite clears the 10.97-times Spot price ratio. Four concurrent A100 shards cut
the missing-trait makespan to 40.92 seconds and reach 19.81 times the N2
throughput, which does clear it. In contrast, four-shard missing-quantitative
throughput is only 3.57 times the N2 result even after A100 utilization reaches
86% over the active window.

Compute Engine bills at least one minute per VM and then bills by the second.
The production projections are all longer than that floor, but several A100
fixture runs are not. For example, the 13.61-second, eight-trait binary fan-out
has a 12.41-times throughput ratio but is billed as 60 seconds; it is therefore
more expensive than the 168.92-second Spot N2 run at this small fixture size.
Throughput break-even and the invoice for a sub-minute experiment are separate
questions.

Production Step 2 covers far more than 700,000 variants, so the table also
reports a phase-based 10-million-variant projection. It holds setup,
prediction reading, null-model fitting, and miscellaneous time fixed, while
scaling genotype input, variant computation, and output by `10m / 700k`. This
is more informative than multiplying the entire wall time: for 32 complete
binary traits, the A100's variant-scaling phases are 14.07 times faster even
though its measured whole-run ratio is 9.79. The projection is not a measured
10-million-variant run and should not be read as one. It also keeps the
measured sample count at 50,000; it is not a forecast for 500,000 samples.

## Production placement boundary

Every timing in this report starts with the genotype files already on the
machine. The 13.61-second A100 result is therefore a local-data compute
diagnostic, not a plausible time to launch a production shard, copy its input,
run it, and collect its output. A 10-100M-variant dataset for 500,000 people is
large enough that localization can dominate a sub-minute compute result. Even
the uncompressed two-bit hardcall payload is 1.25 TB at 10 million variants
and 12.5 TB at 100 million, before metadata; the transferred size depends on
the format and compression achieved by the actual dataset. Naively scaling the
measured fixture's PGEN bytes per genotype gives about 867 GB at 10 million
variants and 8.67 TB at 100 million variants, but production compression may
be different.

The relevant production choices are:

- **Whole-GWAS A100:** run Stages 0 and 1 on the A100, localize the entire Stage
  2 dataset to that machine, and keep the A100 through completion. Multiple
  local REGENIE processes can share the already-local files, but they do not
  remove the initial full-dataset copy.
- **A100-to-N2 handoff:** run Stages 0 and 1 on the A100, then launch Spot N2
  workers for Stage 2. Each worker localizes only its assigned chromosome or
  chromosome group. The shard size should be large enough to amortize startup,
  localization, null-model setup, and the one-minute billing floor, but small
  enough to provide useful parallel wall-time reduction.

The incremental comparison after Stage 1 is approximately
`localize_all + A100_step2` versus
`max(localize_chunk + N2_chunk)` for wall time, and A100 instance time versus
the sum of billed Spot N2 worker times for cost. Aggregate input bytes need not
be replicated in the N2 design when chunks are disjoint, and localization can
happen in parallel. Object-store throughput, disk provisioning, retry behavior,
and Spot preemption will determine the useful fan-out width.

The posted VM rates also exclude persistent-disk charges. A whole-GWAS A100
needs enough local capacity for the complete Stage 2 input, whereas a Spot N2
worker needs only its assigned chunk. That storage difference is not included
in the compute-cost table and is another reason not to interpret it as a final
end-to-end bill.

The compute-only projections above are lower bounds on both architectures;
they do not settle the end-to-end placement question. In particular, a
local-compute cost advantage is not robust evidence for a whole-GWAS A100 when
the alternative localizes smaller chunks on much cheaper workers. A production
decision needs a representative localization benchmark or observed transfer
logs from an actual 10M+ variant dataset.

## Workloads

All comparisons use the same physical PGEN fixture: 50,000 samples and 700,000
variants with real LD structure. Production Step 2 commonly covers 10-100
million variants; 700,000 variants is used here to observe hundreds of blocks
and steady GPU feeding, not as a production-scale elapsed-time claim. Its PGEN
is 6.07 GB and was already on each VM. The repeated trait-matrix runs did not
force a cold page cache, so their genotype-ingest phase is not a cloud
localization measurement. The phenotype panels are:

- 32 quantitative traits, either complete or with per-trait missingness rising
  from 0% to 10%;
- eight binary traits with prevalences from 1% to 50%; and
- eight survival traits with observed-event fractions from 10% to 80%.

The A100 cost follow-up also repeats the binary prevalence and survival event
targets across 32 traits. Complete and missing panels use identical outcomes;
the missing panels inherit the 0-10% quantitative masks and average 5%
missingness. [`prepare_step2_trait_matrix.py`](../prepare_step2_trait_matrix.py)
reproduces all four derived panels. Independent generation on the A100 and N2
machines produced identical SHA-256 checksums.

The N2 full runs use eight physical Xeon cores, eight REGENIE threads, and
oneMKL 2026.1. A100 runs use 12 total host threads. The eight-trait runs use
existing LOCO predictions; the 32-trait binary and survival throughput runs
use `--ignore-pred` on both systems because no matched Stage 1 fits exist for
those synthetic outcomes. T4 runs use four total host threads and
`--ignore-pred`; this gives T4 a small advantage in cross-system elapsed-time
comparisons. The earlier integrated CPU/CUDA pairs read the first 16,000
variants with a block size of 1,000 and use identical options within each
machine.

The original integrated implementation is `94c5d98`; packed host-expansion
elision for complete traits is `ae9144d`, and the missing-trait extension is
`6743323`. Every benchmark binary links to oneMKL.

## Full eight-core CPU anchor

The baseline is `114ef81`; the retained CPU optimization is `db1ce23`.

| Model | Traits | Missingness or correction | Baseline wall | Optimized wall | Throughput |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Quantitative | 32 | 0-10% by trait | 783.62 s | 263.47 s | 2.97x |
| Binary | 8 | approximate Firth | 307.40 s | 252.56 s | 1.22x |
| Survival | 8 | approximate Firth | 433.29 s | 313.80 s | 1.38x |

The quantitative run cuts genotype work from 415.53 to 93.14 seconds and
variant compute from 345.13 to 147.54 seconds. Binary genotype work remains
22.79 seconds while compute falls from 274.28 to 219.61 seconds. Survival
genotype work remains 23.01 seconds while compute falls from 395.78 to 276.51
seconds. Binary and survival Step 2 are plainly not dominated by variant
reading at this trait count.

The CPU implementation avoids work in three places:

- Missing-phenotype accounting and quantitative scoring update only affected
  traits instead of repeatedly applying full sample-by-trait masks.
- Binary scoring uses cached projection crossproducts and materializes a
  sample-length residualized genotype only when a correction is selected.
- Default Cox scoring uses cached Gram and score terms. `--coxscore-exact`
  retains the original calculation, and a residualized genotype is deferred
  until a selected Firth correction needs it.

Complete-case quantitative timing is unchanged in the 16,000-variant CPU
control (5.59 seconds before, 5.55 seconds after). The other pilot results are
recorded in [`2026-07-20-step2.tsv`](2026-07-20-step2.tsv).

On A100, packed-host expansion accounts for most PGEN ingestion time: that
phase falls from 9.27 to 2.31 seconds. The corresponding T4 phase falls from
15.61 to 4.47 seconds. All eight full A100 and T4 output files are
byte-for-byte identical before and after the change. On A100, the
16,000-variant quantitative, binary, and survival validations are also
byte-identical across all 48 files; the T4 binary pilot matches across all
eight files.

Fan-out is an experiment in overlap, not a recommendation to multiply GPU
processes without limit. The four A100 processes divide the chromosomes into
near-equal variant counts and retain 12 total host threads. Their combined CUDA
kernel time is 6.68 seconds, essentially the same work as the single process,
but CPU preparation from one shard overlaps GPU work from another. The T4
reaches saturation with two processes: utilization rises from 64.8% to 90.5%,
yet makespan improves by only 10%. This argues against expecting an enormous
additional T4 gain from transfer or scheduling work alone.

The GPU receives packed PGEN hardcalls directly. Phenotype residuals,
missingness masks, weights, and null-model projection terms stay resident for
the chromosome; only per-block hardcalls go up and small score numerator and
denominator matrices come back. Filtering, result formatting, SPA/Firth, and
exact Cox scoring remain on the CPU. At `ae9144d`, score-only workflows also
skip host-side Eigen genotype materialization that had become dead work.

The current CUDA path is deliberately narrow: additive single-variant hardcall
tests from PGEN, separate phenotype outputs, all analyzed samples present in
the genotype input, and at most 16 covariates. Unsupported modes use the CPU
implementation. Non-PAR blocks that require male dosage rescaling also remain
on the CPU.

## Numerical checks

For the retained CPU optimization, every 16,000-variant pilot is byte-for-byte
identical to `114ef81`, as is the full binary Firth run. In the full
quantitative run, one of 22.4 million rows changes the last printed digit. In
the full survival run, one of 5.6 million rows prints one numeric field at
slightly different precision; beta, standard error, and p-value are unchanged.
The exact Cox control is
byte-for-byte identical.

CPU and CUDA generated identical files for both T4 quantitative panels, the
A100 missing-quantitative panel, all A100 binary and survival panels, and both
T4 survival panels. Two reductions differ only at the output precision limit:

- A100 complete quantitative: one standard error in 512,000 output rows ends
  in `...90` on CPU and `...91` on CUDA; beta, chi-square, and p-value match.
- T4 binary: one exactly null score in 128,000 rows is approximately
  `1.3e-15` on CPU and `1.0e-15` on CUDA. The same row appears in score-only
  and Firth output, but it is not selected for correction.

All other files in those pairs are byte-for-byte identical. No Firth test
failed.

The missing-trait extension has a separate 16,000-variant conformance gate:
32 missing quantitative outputs; 32 quantitative, eight binary, and eight
survival complete outputs; eight binary and eight survival missing outputs;
and 32-trait binary and survival missing outputs. All 160 CPU/CUDA file pairs
are byte-for-byte identical. The full prior/current eight-trait binary and
survival controls add 16 more exact file comparisons. The final A100 CUDA and
oneMKL build passes all three available CTest targets (`step1_compute_cpu`,
`step1_compute_auto`, and `cox_firth`).

## Default behavior and practical conclusion

Removing Step 2 CUDA is not justified, but enabling it broadly would be just as
misleading. It is a packed-PGEN score accelerator with a credible niche in
many-trait binary and survival scans. It is not competitive for quantitative
traits, exact Cox, or correction-heavy analyses. T4 is also not a useful Step
2 placement: two shards reach 90% mean utilization for only a further 10%
wall-time gain, so feeding it better is unlikely to change the production
decision.

The default architecture should remain Stage 0/1 on A100 followed by Spot N2
fan-out for Stage 2. A whole-GWAS A100 is worth revisiting only if a
representative end-to-end run shows that its local score throughput outweighs
the time and storage needed to localize the entire Stage 2 dataset. The
compute-only binary results are strong enough to justify that experiment, not
strong enough to decide it in advance. The survival result is closer to the
cost boundary, and quantitative traits are nowhere near it.

Within a whole-A100 experiment, several local processes are currently the best
way to keep the device fed. They overlap one shard's CPU preparation with
another shard's kernels without multiplying the total host-thread budget. An
internal multi-block pipeline could eventually recover that overlap in one
process, but it would not change the localization boundary and should be judged
on whole-run throughput rather than utilization alone.

In CUDA builds, `--compute-backend auto` makes a per-workflow choice. It keeps
quantitative Step 2 on the CPU for pre-Ampere GPUs while allowing supported
binary and survival scores to use CUDA. Explicit `--compute-backend cuda`
remains useful for measurement and controlled deployment, and
`--step2-profile` reports the backend that actually scored the variants.

The earlier standalone packed-input probe is retained in
[`2026-07-20-step2-cuda.tsv`](2026-07-20-step2-cuda.tsv). It established that
the arithmetic could run on both GPUs, but it substantially overpredicted T4
quantitative and Cox performance because it did not reproduce the integrated
missingness and null-model work. It should be treated as a kernel diagnostic,
not an elapsed-time forecast. The integrated measurements are in
[`2026-07-20-step2-integrated.tsv`](2026-07-20-step2-integrated.tsv). The full
placement, utilization, and fan-out measurements are in
[`2026-07-20-step2-steady-state.tsv`](2026-07-20-step2-steady-state.tsv).
