# Stage 2 at N=500,000 and M=100,000,000

For the production shape considered here, Stage 2 should run on Spot CPU
workers, one chromosome per worker. CUDA makes one A100 faster than one
eight-core N2 worker, but not fast enough to offset the A100 price or the
parallel localization and compute available from 24 CPU workers.

This conclusion is based on direct cold-input runs at **N=500,000,
M=700,000, and 32 traits**. The 100-million-variant projection only scales
those measurements by 2.2-11.5x for an individual chromosome worker. The old
M=16,000 runs are not used for production timing or cost estimates.

## Production comparison

The planning case is deliberately concrete:

| Input or placement | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Samples | 500,000 |
| Stage 2 variants | 100,000,000 |
| Traits | 32 quantitative, binary, or survival traits |
| Comparison scope | Stage 2 after the same A100 Stage 0/1; add that shared time and cost to both whole-GWAS pipelines |
| CPU placement | 24 Spot `n2-standard-16` VMs, eight physical cores used, one VM for each of chromosomes 1-22, X, and Y |
| GPU placement | One on-demand `a2-highgpu-1g`, one REGENIE process, all 12 host vCPUs available |
| Data placement | Object storage and compute in the same region; appropriately sized temporary disks |
| Variant allocation | Proportional to GRCh38 chromosome length |
| Temporary disk | About 753 GiB for the chromosome 1 CPU worker and 9.12 TiB for the whole-scan A100 placement; both include 10% headroom for input plus results |

An `a2-highgpu-1g` supports at most eight 375 GiB Local SSD devices, 2.93 TiB
in total, so Local SSD cannot hold the projected whole-scan input. The A100
model therefore uses a capacity-appropriate balanced Persistent Disk.

The CPU wall time is the completion time of the slowest chromosome, which is
chromosome 1 with 8.06 million projected variants. CPU cost is the sum across
all 24 workers; it has already been multiplied by the worker count. Each
worker is deleted when its chromosome finishes.

| 32-trait model | Missingness | 24 Spot N2 wall | Total 24-worker cost | Optimistic one-A100 wall | Total A100 cost |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Quantitative | none | 1.93 h | $9.03 | 9.07 h | $44.94 |
| Quantitative | 0-10% | 5.62 h | $25.64 | 28.94 h | $143.33 |
| Binary | none | 3.71 h | $17.02 | 11.28 h | $55.87 |
| Binary | 0-10% | 5.51 h | $25.15 | 12.60 h | $62.40 |
| Survival | none | 5.01 h | $22.88 | 26.84 h | $132.92 |
| Survival | 0-10% | 7.43 h | $33.78 | 29.46 h | $145.76 |

“Total cost” includes VM time and temporary zonal Persistent Disk for the
whole placement. Same-region Cloud Storage transfer is free. The A100 column
is intentionally favorable to CUDA: it uses the documented 800 MiB/s maximum
write rate for this machine type and assumes that input localization and
result upload overlap perfectly with association compute. The CPU model
localizes one chromosome, runs REGENIE, uploads its result, and then deletes
the worker. Scratch capacity is 110% of projected PGEN plus result size.
Neither side includes scheduler startup, LOCO prediction transfer, Spot
retries, or association-test corrections.

Long-lived object-storage charges for the source data and final results are
not included because they are the same for both placements.

Prices captured on 2026-07-20 are $3.673385/hour for one on-demand
`a2-highgpu-1g`, $0.254688/hour for a co-located Spot `n2-standard-16`,
$0.000136986/GiB-hour for balanced Persistent Disk, and
$0.000232877/GiB-hour for SSD Persistent Disk. The A100 is 14.42x the hourly
price of one Spot N2 before disks. A cheaper `n2-highcpu-16` is not used in the
headline model because its 16 GiB of RAM leaves little operating headroom over
the measured 12.8 GiB peak process RSS.

## Why localization changes the wall time

The N=500,000 real-LD PGEN occupies 61.51 GB for 700,000 variants, or 87,868
bytes per variant. At the same density, 100 million variants occupy 7.99 TiB.
This is a planning estimate; compression varies somewhat with allele
frequency.

The durable same-region localization measurement copied a 6.98 GiB PGEN and
included `sync` in the timed interval. It took 13.28 seconds on
`n2-standard-16` with SSD Persistent Disk: 0.526 GiB/s end to end.

| Placement | Data localized by the slowest unit | Localization wall |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Chromosome 1 on one N2 worker | 659.69 GiB | 20.9 min at the observed rate |
| All 24 chromosomes on one A100 | 7.99 TiB | at least 174.6 min at the documented 800 MiB/s disk limit |

The N2 fleet still transfers the same aggregate 7.99 TiB, but it does so in
parallel. The one-worker-per-chromosome estimate requires about 12.6 GiB/s of
aggregate object-store throughput if every worker sustains the measured rate.
That fleet-wide rate has not been measured. Even at one quarter of the
observed per-worker rate, chromosome 1 localization would take 83.7 minutes,
still below the A100's optimistic 174.6-minute floor.

The chromosome allocation is not balanced: chromosome 1 receives 8.06 million
variants, chromosome 2 receives 7.84 million, and chromosome 21 receives 1.51
million. The wall-time model uses the largest worker rather than the
4.17-million-variant arithmetic mean. X and Y are included; their score rates
are projected from autosomal measurements. Smaller chromosome workers use the
documented throughput for their capacity-sized SSD Persistent Disks; they
still finish before chromosome 1 and their lower rate is included in total
fleet cost.

## Direct N=500,000, M=700,000 measurements

Every row below scans the same 500,000-sample, 700,000-variant real-LD-derived
PGEN with 32 traits, one categorical covariate, `--bsize 1000`, and
`--ignore-pred`. The PGEN was read from disk in each retained run. N2 uses
oneMKL and eight physical cores. A100 uses CUDA, oneMKL, all 12 host vCPUs,
and one REGENIE process.

| Model | Missingness | N2 wall | A100 wall | A100 speedup | Mean GPU util. | Mean GPU power |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Quantitative | none | 497.84 s | 238.65 s | 2.09x | 80.3% | 274 W |
| Quantitative | 0-10% | 1,650.04 s | 744.02 s | 2.22x | 91.1% | 333 W |
| Binary | none | 1,056.65 s | 303.47 s | 3.48x | 81.4% | 277 W |
| Binary | 0-10% | 1,622.77 s | 339.55 s | 4.78x | 82.6% | 261 W |
| Survival | none | 1,477.28 s | 718.44 s | 2.06x | 88.7% | 323 W |
| Survival | 0-10% | 2,236.61 s | 786.03 s | 2.85x | 88.7% | 307 W |

The accelerator is not severely underutilized: across the six standard cold
runs, mean GPU utilization is 80.3-91.1% and mean power is 261-333 W. In the
complete quantitative panel, giving the A100 host side 12 threads instead of
eight changes wall time by less than one second. Increasing `--bsize` from
1,000 to 2,000 or 4,000 makes the cold run 1.2% or 3.8% slower, respectively.
The measured single-worker speedup is far below the 14.42x hourly price ratio,
and one A100 cannot reproduce the wall-time advantage of 24 independent
localization and compute paths.

## Trait panels

The quantitative panel contains 32 correlated traits. Its missing-data version
increases per-trait missingness linearly from 0% to 10%.

Binary prevalences cycle through 1%, 2%, 5%, 10%, 20%, 30%, 40%, and 50%.
Survival event fractions cycle through 10% to 80% in ten-point increments.
The missing binary and survival panels reuse the quantitative missingness
masks, so each outcome has between 0% and 10% missing observations.

These are score-only runs. Approximate Firth and other selected-test
corrections still execute on the CPU. In the earlier N=50,000, M=700,000
eight-trait correction benchmark, A100 binary Firth was only 1.26x faster than
the N2 and T4 was slower than the N2. A correction-heavy production scan will
therefore be slower than the headline estimates and is less favorable to
CUDA, not more.

## How Stage 2 scales with sample count

The 700,000-variant runs also give a direct N=50,000 to N=500,000 comparison
for the variant-compute phase on the current CPU implementation:

| Model | Missingness | Variant-compute multiplier for 10x N |
| --- | --- | ---: |
| Quantitative | none | 8.54x |
| Quantitative | 0-10% | 9.32x |
| Binary | none | 9.21x |
| Binary | 0-10% | 9.32x |
| Survival | none | 9.30x |
| Survival | 0-10% | 9.34x |

There is no single multiplier for total wall time: setup and output are mostly
fixed, while null-model fitting and genotype I/O scale differently from score
computation. For a large variant count, multiplying the N=50,000 score time by
roughly 9 is a useful planning rule, but multiplying an entire short run by 10
is not. The production table above avoids this extrapolation because all of
its compute rates were measured directly at N=500,000.

The retained CPU implementation prepares phenotype and null-model terms once
and scores genotype blocks with oneMKL. At N=50,000 and M=700,000 it improves
32-trait binary and survival score scans by 4.5-6.6x and quantitative scans by
1.35-1.40x relative to the earlier CPU path. Exact output comparisons are
byte-identical for most files; the largest printed CPU/CUDA difference in the
validated panels is about 1e-6.

The matched upstream v4.1.2 check at N=500,000, M=16,000, and one quantitative
trait took 27.99 seconds on eight N2 cores versus 7.58 seconds for the retained
CPU path. That small check establishes the upstream comparison, but it is not
used to forecast a production scan.

## Decision and limitations

CPU fan-out is the production Stage 2 path. The current CUDA backend is a
valid single-process accelerator, but it is not competitive with co-located
Spot N2 workers on either wall time or cost. T4 has no current Stage 2
placement case. Depending on the trait panel, the projected A100 wall is
2.29-5.36x longer and its cost is 2.48-5.81x higher. There is no obvious
utilization or block-size fix that closes that gap. CUDA should remain clearly
marked experimental if there is an active roadmap for a multi-fold
single-process improvement; otherwise, removing the Stage 2 CUDA path would
reduce maintenance burden and user confusion without removing a competitive
production option.

The 100-million-variant values are projections, not measured whole-genome
runs. They assume phase-linear scaling from M=700,000, chromosome-length
variant density, sufficient aggregate object-store bandwidth, and a temporary
disk deleted with each worker. They do not model VM startup, preemption,
different sex-chromosome tests, LOCO prediction parsing, or variation in the
rare-variant and correction mix. Measured 32-trait output is about 2.1 GiB at
M=700,000, implying roughly 277-308 GiB at M=100 million; result upload is
included in the cost model using the same throughput assumptions as input
localization.
