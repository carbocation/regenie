# Step 2 on CPU, T4, and A100: 2026-07-20

The CPU changes remain the most broadly useful Step 2 improvement. On an
eight-core N2, the full 700,000-variant run is 2.97x faster for 32 quantitative
traits with variable missingness, 1.22x faster for eight binary traits with
approximate Firth correction, and 1.38x faster for eight survival traits with
approximate Firth correction.

CUDA is worthwhile for some, but not all, multi-trait readout workloads. In the
matched 16,000-variant runs, binary score testing is 2.41x faster on T4 and
1.81x faster on A100. Survival score testing is 1.30x and 1.84x faster,
respectively. CPU-side Firth corrections reduce those gains to 1.24-1.45x.
Quantitative CUDA is neutral to modestly faster on A100 and substantially worse
on T4. The default `auto` policy therefore keeps quantitative Step 2 on the CPU
for pre-Ampere GPUs while still using T4 for binary and survival scoring.

This is not a case where maximizing GPU utilization is the right objective.
The T4 reaches 83% mean utilization during the missing-quantitative scoring
interval and takes twice as long as the CPU. Its much less saturated binary
kernel produces the largest wall-time improvement.

## Workloads

All comparisons use the same physical PGEN fixture: 50,000 samples and 700,000
variants with real LD structure. The phenotype panels are:

- 32 quantitative traits, either complete or with per-trait missingness rising
  from 0% to 10%;
- eight binary traits with prevalences from 1% to 50%; and
- eight survival traits with observed-event fractions from 10% to 80%.

The N2 full runs use eight physical Xeon cores, eight REGENIE threads, and
oneMKL 2026.1. The integrated GPU comparisons read the first 16,000 variants
with a block size of 1,000. A100 runs use 12 threads and existing LOCO
predictions. T4 runs use four threads and `--ignore-pred`; the CPU and CUDA
member of every pair use identical options. Absolute A100 and T4 times should
therefore not be compared to each other, but the within-system CPU/CUDA ratios
are matched.

The integrated implementation is `94c5d98`. Timings were collected from its
final candidate; the last changes added automatic T4 quantitative fallback,
corrected profiling labels, and handled flipped missing genotypes and non-PAR
dosage compensation. Those changes do not affect the timed autosomal fixtures.
Every binary links to oneMKL.

## Full eight-core CPU anchor

The baseline is `114ef81`; the retained CPU optimization is `db1ce23`.

| Model | Traits | Missingness or correction | Baseline wall | Optimized wall | Throughput |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Quantitative | 32 | 0-10% by trait | 783.62 s | 263.47 s | 2.97x |
| Binary | 8 | approximate Firth | 307.40 s | 252.56 s | 1.22x |
| Survival | 8 | approximate Firth | 433.29 s | 313.80 s | 1.38x |

The quantitative run cuts genotype work from 415.53 to 93.14 seconds and
variant compute from 345.13 to 147.54 seconds. Binary genotype work remains
22.79 seconds while compute falls from 274.28 to 219.61 seconds. Survival
genotype work remains 23.01 seconds while compute falls from 395.78 to 276.51
seconds. Binary and survival Step 2 are plainly not dominated by variant
reading at this trait count.

The CPU implementation avoids work in three places:

- Missing-phenotype accounting and quantitative scoring update only affected
  traits instead of repeatedly applying full sample-by-trait masks.
- Binary scoring uses cached projection crossproducts and materializes a
  sample-length residualized genotype only when a correction is selected.
- Default Cox scoring uses cached Gram and score terms. `--coxscore-exact`
  retains the original calculation, and a residualized genotype is deferred
  until a selected Firth correction needs it.

Complete-case quantitative timing is unchanged in the 16,000-variant CPU
control (5.59 seconds before, 5.55 seconds after). The other pilot results are
recorded in [`2026-07-20-step2.tsv`](2026-07-20-step2.tsv).

## Integrated CUDA result

These are end-to-end elapsed times, including PGEN handling, null-model setup,
selective corrections, and result output. A speedup greater than 1 means CUDA
reduced wall time.

| T4, four CPU threads | Quantitative, 32 complete traits | 6.48 s | 7.29 s | 0.89x |
| T4, four CPU threads | Quantitative, 32 traits, 0-10% missing | 15.35 s | 30.60 s | 0.50x |
| T4, four CPU threads | Binary, eight traits, score only | 10.81 s | 4.48 s | 2.41x |
| T4, four CPU threads | Binary, eight traits, approximate Firth | 25.34 s | 18.32 s | 1.38x |
| T4, four CPU threads | Survival, eight traits, score only | 14.18 s | 10.88 s | 1.30x |
| T4, four CPU threads | Survival, eight traits, approximate Firth | 26.90 s | 21.68 s | 1.24x |
| A100, 12 CPU threads | Quantitative, 32 complete traits | 5.87 s | 5.82 s | 1.01x |
| A100, 12 CPU threads | Quantitative, 32 traits, 0-10% missing | 9.97 s | 8.83 s | 1.13x |
| A100, 12 CPU threads | Binary, eight traits, score only | 6.82 s | 3.77 s | 1.81x |
| A100, 12 CPU threads | Binary, eight traits, approximate Firth | 15.76 s | 11.97 s | 1.32x |
| A100, 12 CPU threads | Survival, eight traits, score only | 8.32 s | 4.51 s | 1.84x |
| A100, 12 CPU threads | Survival, eight traits, approximate Firth | 16.52 s | 11.37 s | 1.45x |

The GPU receives packed PGEN hardcalls directly. Phenotype residuals,
missingness masks, weights, and null-model projection terms stay resident for
the chromosome; only per-block hardcalls go up and small score numerator and
denominator matrices come back. Filtering, result formatting, SPA/Firth, and
exact Cox scoring remain on the CPU. Score-only workflows also skip host-side
Eigen genotype materialization that has become dead work.

The current CUDA path is deliberately narrow: additive single-variant hardcall
tests from PGEN, separate phenotype outputs, all analyzed samples present in
the genotype input, and at most 16 covariates. Unsupported modes use the CPU
implementation. Non-PAR blocks that require male dosage rescaling also remain
on the CPU.

Telemetry was sampled every 250 ms. That is too coarse to characterize the
subsecond A100 binary and survival kernels, but it is sufficient to answer the
main question. The A100 finishes those kernels too quickly to look saturated
over the full run. The T4 missing-quantitative kernel is genuinely busy and
still loses badly. Occupancy and power are diagnostic signals, not acceptance
criteria.

Firth makes the distinction especially clear. CUDA still accelerates the
initial score pass, but thousands of selected corrections execute on the CPU.
That is why T4 binary falls from a 2.41x score-only gain to 1.38x with Firth,
and why whole-run GPU utilization falls with it. Moving the score calculation
alone cannot remove that correction cost.

## Numerical checks

For the retained CPU optimization, every 16,000-variant pilot is byte-for-byte
identical to `114ef81`, as is the full binary Firth run. In the full
quantitative run, one of 22.4 million rows changes the last printed digit. In
the full survival run, one of 5.6 million rows prints one numeric field at
slightly different precision; beta, standard error, and p-value are unchanged.
The exact Cox control is
byte-for-byte identical.

CPU and CUDA generated identical files for both T4 quantitative panels, the
A100 missing-quantitative panel, all A100 binary and survival panels, and both
T4 survival panels. Two reductions differ only at the output precision limit:

- A100 complete quantitative: one standard error in 512,000 output rows ends
  in `...90` on CPU and `...91` on CUDA; beta, chi-square, and p-value match.
- T4 binary: one exactly null score in 128,000 rows is approximately
  `1.3e-15` on CPU and `1.0e-15` on CUDA. The same row appears in score-only
  and Firth output, but it is not selected for correction.

All other files in those pairs are byte-for-byte identical. No Firth test
failed. The final source builds and passes all three available CTest targets on
the eight-core CPU, T4, and A100 configurations.

## Default behavior and practical conclusion

In CUDA builds, `--compute-backend auto` now makes a per-workflow Step 2
choice. A final acceptance run selected CPU for T4 quantitative scoring, CUDA
for T4 binary scoring, and CUDA for A100 quantitative scoring. Explicit
`--compute-backend cuda` remains available for measurement and experimentation.
The profile reports the backend that actually scored the variants.

There is no enormous universal CUDA win for Step 2. There is a useful GPU path
for multi-trait binary readout on both GPUs and for survival readout on A100;
T4 survival is helpful but less compelling. A100 quantitative missingness gets
a modest gain, while T4 quantitative should stay on the CPU. For distributed
fan-out, T4 is credible for binary traits and possibly survival traits, but not
for the quantitative panel tested here. For a single-machine A100 analysis,
Step 2 CUDA can shorten binary and survival readout, but CPU setup, PGEN work,
output, and corrections keep it from approaching a whole-GWAS-in-seconds
result.

The earlier standalone packed-input probe is retained in
[`2026-07-20-step2-cuda.tsv`](2026-07-20-step2-cuda.tsv). It established that
the arithmetic could run on both GPUs, but it substantially overpredicted T4
quantitative and Cox performance because it did not reproduce the integrated
missingness and null-model work. It should be treated as a kernel diagnostic,
not an elapsed-time forecast. The integrated measurements are in
[`2026-07-20-step2-integrated.tsv`](2026-07-20-step2-integrated.tsv).
