# Step 2 on CPU, T4, and A100: 2026-07-20

CPU fan-out is the production default for Stage 2. A new blockwise oneMKL path
makes the eight-core CPU 1.35-6.61 times faster across the measured 32-trait
workloads, and a packed PGEN decoder reduces the remaining missing-trait input
cost. The A100 is still faster than one eight-core N2, but none of the 32-trait
rows clears even the 4.73-times on-demand price ratio, much less the
10.97-times Spot ratio. Cheap workers can also localize disjoint chromosome
chunks instead of requiring the entire association dataset on the A100.

This report originally treated four REGENIE processes sharing one A100 as the
best GPU result. That was a benchmarking mistake: it demonstrated missing
concurrency in the library, not the placement users should adopt. Revision
`2e14995` moves that concurrency into one process. It overlaps PGEN ingestion,
GPU scoring, and host-side result work; retains chromosome-invariant null state
instead of rebuilding it 22 times; and prefetches LOCO predictions when doing
so improves elapsed time. The old multi-process measurements remain only as a
diagnostic ceiling.

Revision `8953759` performs CPU scores for a whole genotype block with matrix
multiplication rather than thousands of small per-variant operations. On the
50,000-sample, 700,000-variant fixture, 32-trait CPU time is now 68.26-188.30
seconds for quantitative traits, 109.52-168.75 seconds for binary traits, and
154.27-228.50 seconds for survival traits. Eight-trait score-only and
approximate-Firth workloads improve as well. The existing packed single-trait
path is retained because materializing a dense block would make that workload
slower and use more memory.

## Cost model

Google Cloud's posted prices on 2026-07-20 are $3.673385/hour for an on-demand
`a2-highgpu-1g` in `us-central1`, $0.776944/hour for an on-demand
`n2-standard-16` in `us-west1`, and $0.335008/hour for the same N2 shape as a
Spot VM. The A100 must therefore be 4.73 times faster to break even against
on-demand N2 and 10.97 times faster against Spot N2. These are complete VM
prices; the A2 price includes its A100, 12 vCPUs, and 85 GiB of memory. The N2
is billed as a 16-vCPU VM even though SMT was disabled to expose eight physical
cores during measurement.

The primary comparison below is on-demand A100 versus Spot N2. At 700,000
variants, the A100 is 1.81-2.40 times faster for 32 quantitative traits,
3.20-4.68 times faster for 32 binary traits, and 2.36-3.46 times faster for 32
survival traits. Every 32-trait ratio is below on-demand break-even and far
below Spot break-even. The eight-trait LOCO rows range from 2.14 to 5.22 times
faster on the A100; one missing-binary row clears on-demand break-even, but all
four remain well below Spot break-even.

Compute Engine bills at least one minute per VM and then bills by the second.
The production projections are all longer than that floor, but several A100
fixture runs are not. Throughput break-even and the invoice for a sub-minute
experiment are therefore separate questions.

Production Step 2 covers far more than 700,000 variants, so the table also
reports a phase-based 10-million-variant projection. It holds setup,
prediction reading, null-model fitting, and miscellaneous time fixed, while
scaling genotype input, variant computation, and output by `10m / 700k`. This
is more informative than multiplying the entire wall time because the fixed
setup becomes less important as the variant count grows. The projection is
not a measured 10-million-variant run and should not be read as one. It also
keeps the measured sample count at 50,000; it is not a forecast for 500,000
samples.

## Production placement boundary

Every timing in this report starts with the genotype files already on the
machine. A sub-minute A100 fixture result is not a plausible time to launch a
production shard, copy its input, run it, and collect its output. A
10-100M-variant dataset for 500,000 people is large enough that localization
can dominate a sub-minute compute result. Even
the uncompressed two-bit hardcall payload is 1.25 TB at 10 million variants
and 12.5 TB at 100 million, before metadata; the transferred size depends on
the format and compression achieved by the actual dataset. Naively scaling the
measured fixture's PGEN bytes per genotype gives about 867 GB at 10 million
variants and 8.67 TB at 100 million variants, but production compression may
be different.

The relevant production choices are:

- **Whole-GWAS A100:** run Stages 0 and 1 on the A100, localize the entire Stage
  2 dataset to that machine, and keep the A100 through completion. The measured
  GPU path uses one REGENIE process.
- **A100-to-N2 handoff:** run Stages 0 and 1 on the A100, then launch Spot N2
  workers for Stage 2. Each worker localizes only its assigned chromosome or
  chromosome group. The shard size should be large enough to amortize startup,
  localization, null-model setup, and the one-minute billing floor, but small
  enough to provide useful parallel wall-time reduction.

The incremental comparison after Stage 1 is approximately
`localize_all + A100_step2` versus
`max(localize_chunk + N2_chunk)` for wall time, and A100 instance time versus
the sum of billed Spot N2 worker times for cost. Aggregate input bytes need not
be replicated in the N2 design when chunks are disjoint, and localization can
happen in parallel. Object-store throughput, disk provisioning, retry behavior,
and Spot preemption will determine the useful fan-out width.

The posted VM rates also exclude persistent-disk charges. A whole-GWAS A100
needs enough local capacity for the complete Stage 2 input, whereas a Spot N2
worker needs only its assigned chunk. That storage difference is not included
in the compute-cost table and is another reason not to interpret it as a final
end-to-end bill.

The compute-only projections are lower bounds on both architectures and retain
the measured 50,000-sample size, so they are not a substitute for an observed
500,000-sample production run. They do settle the direction of the current
placement decision: the CPU design is already faster and cheaper before adding
the A100's whole-dataset localization and storage burden. Transfer logs from an
actual 10M+ variant dataset are still useful for choosing N2 shard size and
fan-out width.

## Workloads

All comparisons use the same physical PGEN fixture: 50,000 samples and 700,000
variants with real LD structure. Production Step 2 commonly covers 10-100
million variants; 700,000 variants is used here to observe hundreds of blocks
and steady GPU feeding, not as a production-scale elapsed-time claim. Its PGEN
is 6.07 GB and was already on each VM. The repeated trait-matrix runs did not
force a cold page cache, so their genotype-ingest phase is not a cloud
localization measurement. The phenotype panels are:

- 32 quantitative traits, either complete or with per-trait missingness rising
  from 0% to 10%;
- eight binary traits with prevalences from 1% to 50%; and
- eight survival traits with observed-event fractions from 10% to 80%.

The A100 cost follow-up also repeats the binary prevalence and survival event
targets across 32 traits. Complete and missing panels use identical outcomes;
the missing panels inherit the 0-10% quantitative masks and average 5%
missingness. [`prepare_step2_trait_matrix.py`](../prepare_step2_trait_matrix.py)
reproduces all four derived panels. Independent generation on the A100 and N2
machines produced identical SHA-256 checksums.

The N2 full runs use eight physical Xeon cores, eight REGENIE threads, and
oneMKL 2026.1. A100 runs use 12 total host threads. The eight-trait runs use
existing LOCO predictions; the 32-trait binary and survival throughput runs
use `--ignore-pred` on both systems because no matched Stage 1 fits exist for
those synthetic outcomes. T4 runs use four total host threads and
`--ignore-pred`; this gives T4 a small advantage in cross-system elapsed-time
comparisons. The earlier integrated CPU/CUDA pairs read the first 16,000
variants with a block size of 1,000 and use identical options within each
machine.

The original integrated implementation is `94c5d98`; packed host-expansion
elision for complete traits is `ae9144d`, and the missing-trait extension is
`6743323`. The single-process CUDA pipeline is `2e14995`, and the blockwise CPU
implementation is `8953759`. Every benchmark binary links to oneMKL.

## Full eight-core CPU anchor

The original baseline is `114ef81`, the first retained CPU pass is `db1ce23`,
and blockwise scoring plus packed missing-trait decode is `8953759`.

| Model | Traits | Missingness or correction | Original | First CPU pass | Current | Overall speedup |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Quantitative | 32 | 0-10% by trait | 783.62 s | 263.47 s | 188.30 s | 4.16x |
| Binary | 8 | approximate Firth | 307.40 s | 252.56 s | 144.31 s | 2.13x |
| Survival | 8 | approximate Firth | 433.29 s | 313.80 s | 132.35 s | 3.27x |

The current implementation prepares phenotype and null-model terms once, then
uses matrix multiplication to score every variant in a genotype block. It is
enabled for quantitative panels with at least 16 traits and binary or survival
panels with at least four traits. Smaller quantitative panels retain the packed
per-variant path, which is faster for one trait and avoids a dense genotype
block. Firth and SPA still materialize a genotype only for selected corrections;
the initial score pass uses the block backend.

For missing-trait PGEN input, the hardcalls are now decoded from packed bytes
before the existing phenotype-specific allele-count bookkeeping. On the full
32-trait panels this reduces genotype time from 93.26 to 68.01 seconds for
quantitative traits, 94.92 to 68.89 seconds for binary traits, and 107.95 to
84.67 seconds for survival traits. Every output from the decoder-only
comparison is byte-identical.

The block backend has its own deterministic conformance test for complete and
missing quantitative scores, binary scores, and Cox scores. The full binary
Firth run selects 59,904 corrections and is byte-identical to the prior result.
The survival run selects 2,827,678 corrections; seven files are byte-identical,
and one chi-square value changes from `0.988900` to `0.988899`.

## Historical saturation diagnostic

Before the single-process pipeline, splitting one eight-trait binary job over
four REGENIE processes reduced A100 wall time from 27.29 to 13.61 seconds. That
experiment showed that CPU preparation could overlap GPU work; it was not an
appropriate production recommendation or cost-model input. Revision `2e14995`
implements the overlap within REGENIE and is the basis for every headline and
projection above.

The T4 diagnostic reached 90.5% utilization with two processes but improved
wall time only from 60.93 to 54.77 seconds. That remains evidence against a
large T4 opportunity from scheduling alone. The historical measurements are
retained in `2026-07-20-step2-steady-state.tsv` for reproducibility.

The matched correction result is:

| Placement | Prediction mode | Corrected tests | Wall | vs. N2 | GPU util. |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Eight-core N2 | LOCO | 59,904 | 144.31 s | 1.00x | n/a |
| A100 | LOCO | 59,904 | 114.92 s | 1.26x | 6.5% |
| T4 | ignored | 57,990 | 219.41 s | 0.66x | 25.7% |

All three use approximate Firth with `--pThresh 0.01`. The A100 and N2
correction counts match. T4 omits predictions, which changes its scores and
selects fewer corrections, so its row is slightly advantaged and not a strict
statistical match. The A100's 1.26-times speedup is far below either price
break-even. Low utilization is expected because the correction path is still
CPU-only.

The GPU receives packed PGEN hardcalls directly. Phenotype residuals,

tests from PGEN, separate phenotype outputs, all analyzed samples present in
the genotype input, and at most 16 covariates. Unsupported modes use the CPU
implementation. Non-PAR blocks that require male dosage rescaling also remain
on the CPU.

## Numerical checks

For the retained CPU optimization, every 16,000-variant pilot is byte-for-byte
identical to `114ef81`, as is the full binary Firth run. In the full
quantitative run, one of 22.4 million rows changes the last printed digit. In
the full survival run, one of 5.6 million rows prints one numeric field at
slightly different precision; beta, standard error, and p-value are unchanged.
The exact Cox control is
byte-for-byte identical.

The blockwise follow-up compares every row of the six full 32-trait outputs
with `db1ce23`. There are no structural differences. Maximum beta differences
are at most `1e-7`, and the largest chi-square or printed log-p difference is
about `1e-6`; most files are byte-identical. Replacing the missing-trait decoder
afterward produces byte-identical files in all 32 quantitative, 32 binary, 32
survival, eight binary, and eight survival comparisons. The one-trait,
500,000-sample control also remains byte-identical and retains its packed path.

CPU and CUDA generated identical files for both T4 quantitative panels, the
A100 missing-quantitative panel, all A100 binary and survival panels, and both
T4 survival panels. Two reductions differ only at the output precision limit:

- A100 complete quantitative: one standard error in 512,000 output rows ends
  in `...90` on CPU and `...91` on CUDA; beta, chi-square, and p-value match.
- T4 binary: one exactly null score in 128,000 rows is approximately
  `1.3e-15` on CPU and `1.0e-15` on CUDA. The same row appears in score-only
  and Firth output, but it is not selected for correction.

All other files in those pairs are byte-for-byte identical. No Firth test
failed.

The missing-trait extension has a separate 16,000-variant conformance gate:
32 missing quantitative outputs; 32 quantitative, eight binary, and eight
survival complete outputs; eight binary and eight survival missing outputs;
and 32-trait binary and survival missing outputs. All 160 CPU/CUDA file pairs
are byte-for-byte identical. The full prior/current eight-trait binary and
survival controls add 16 more exact file comparisons. The final A100 CUDA and
oneMKL build passes all four CTest targets (`step1_compute_cpu`,
`step1_compute_auto`, `step2_compute_cpu`, and `cox_firth`); the CPU-only
oneMKL build passes the same four targets.

The final single-process pipeline adds 224 exact full-fixture comparisons: 32
outputs for each complete and missing quantitative, binary, and survival panel,
plus eight outputs for each complete and missing binary and survival LOCO
panel.

## Default behavior and practical conclusion

Stage 2 CUDA is not currently a placement argument. It accelerates supported
scores when the data and an A100 are already resident, but its 1.81-4.68-times
advantage over one eight-core N2 does not pay for the VM. Removing it now would
discard a tested local accelerator without improving CPU performance, so it
should remain an explicitly scoped backend rather than be presented as the
production default. It is not competitive for exact Cox or correction-heavy
analyses. T4 is also not a useful Step 2 placement: two shards reach 90% mean
utilization for only a further 10% wall-time gain.

The default architecture is Stage 0/1 on A100 followed by Spot N2 fan-out for
Stage 2. In the 10-million-variant projection, 22 ideal N2 workers are 4.06-
8.60 times faster and 2.03-4.29 times cheaper than keeping the A100, before
accounting for the A100's less favorable localization and storage shape. A
whole-GWAS A100 should be reconsidered only after another large algorithmic
gain, not merely higher GPU utilization.

Within a whole-A100 experiment, use one REGENIE process. The internal pipeline
now beats the former multi-process diagnostic for the 32-trait binary and
survival panels and nearly matches it for 32 quantitative traits. The remaining
eight-trait LOCO gap is concentrated in per-chromosome null-model work; any
future change should be judged on whole-run throughput and exact numerical
behavior, not utilization alone.

In CUDA builds, `--compute-backend auto` makes a per-workflow choice. It keeps
quantitative Step 2 on the CPU for pre-Ampere GPUs while allowing supported
binary and survival scores to use CUDA. Explicit `--compute-backend cuda`
remains useful for measurement and controlled deployment, and
`--step2-profile` reports the backend that actually scored the variants.

The earlier standalone packed-input probe is retained in
[`2026-07-20-step2-cuda.tsv`](2026-07-20-step2-cuda.tsv). It established that
the arithmetic could run on both GPUs, but it substantially overpredicted T4
quantitative and Cox performance because it did not reproduce the integrated
missingness and null-model work. It should be treated as a kernel diagnostic,
not an elapsed-time forecast. The integrated measurements are in
[`2026-07-20-step2-integrated.tsv`](2026-07-20-step2-integrated.tsv). The full
placement, utilization, and fan-out measurements are in
[`2026-07-20-step2-steady-state.tsv`](2026-07-20-step2-steady-state.tsv).
